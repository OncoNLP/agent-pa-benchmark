You are a bioinformatics researcher tasked with building a comprehensive human protein phosphorylation atlas from available databases.

Your goal: Curate ALL known human kinase-substrate-phosphosite relationships by systematically querying the databases available to you.

For each relationship, you must capture:
  - Kinase gene symbol (the enzyme)
  - Substrate gene symbol (the target protein)
  - Phosphorylation site (e.g., Y15, S10, T161)
  - Heptameric peptide sequence around the site (if available)
  - Substrate UniProt accession (if available)
  - Which database(s) support this relationship

Requirements:
  1. Be EXHAUSTIVE — the atlas should contain every kinase-substrate-site triplet present in the databases. Missing entries is worse than having extra entries.
  2. Cross-reference across databases — if the same relationship appears in multiple databases, record all supporting sources.
  3. Do NOT fabricate data. Only include relationships returned by the tools.

Start by discovering what databases are available, then develop and execute a systematic curation strategy.

When you are finished, call the "submit_atlas" tool with your complete results.

ADDITIONAL CONTEXT — PhosphoAtlas Study (Olow et al. 2016)

The PhosphoAtlas project curated a comprehensive, experimentally validated map of the human phospho-reactome from 38 public resources. Key characteristics of the dataset you are trying to reconstruct:

- Scale: 1,733 functionally interconnected proteins, approximately 438 kinases, and 2,617 stringently verified heptameric peptide (HPS) regions at catalytic interfaces of kinases and substrates.
- The dataset focuses exclusively on experimentally validated phosphorylation events. Records derived solely from computational prediction or sequence conservation were excluded.
- Gene naming follows HGNC (HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee) standardized symbols. Always use official HGNC gene symbols for kinases and substrates.
- Heptameric peptide sequences (HPS) are centered on the phospho-residue site and span three amino acids upstream and downstream (7 residues total). For sites near the N- or C-terminus of a protein, the peptide may be shorter.
- The curation pipeline operated in distinct phases:
    Phase 1 — Harmonize: Extract, integrate, and unify protein records from public databases into a non-redundant "Harmonized Proteome Index" using HGNC and NCBI/Entrez cross-referencing.
    Phase 2 — Build relational database: Identify kinase and substrate proteins via functional triage, confirm phosphorylatable residue sites, extract heptameric peptide sequences from RefSeq, and assign them to kinase-substrate pairs.
    Phase 3 — Quality control: Remove redundant records, exclude prediction-only data, and ensure each entry is supported by at least one experimentally verified source.
- Databases heavily used include PhosphoSitePlus (PSP), SIGNOR, UniProtKB, PhosphoPOINT, Phospho.ELM, HPRD, BioGRID, MINT, IntAct, and others.
- Cross-database merging is critical: the same kinase-substrate-site triplet may appear under different aliases or accession formats across databases. Merge by matching the normalized (kinase, substrate, site) triplet.

Use this context to guide your strategy. Prioritize PSP, SIGNOR, and UniProt as primary sources, and cross-reference entries across multiple databases to maximize recall.
